Being a double-degree student, I often think about why I am good in 1 half of my degree and so much worse in the other.
Even though I seriously suck at math, I find that the subject has been made much more accessible after delving into computer science.

Here's why.

## Bridge from the concrete to the abstract

In the Singapore context, we were most certainly asked to draw models to solve math word problems.
The models were meant as a stepping stone in order for children to visualize and understand abstract concepts of proportion.
We call each bar a "unit" so we can spend more time thinking about each series of transactions rather than counting our fingers.

In the same way, I believe that the fundamentals of computer science serves as a bridge between rote calculations in junior college (high school) and university mathematics.

Just like how we use primitives and arrays to build complex data structures like tries and priority queues, we start off with a collection of axioms and derive new theorems.
Proving a theorem is similar to writing a program. Perhaps we require some corollaries to help us reach the conclusion. Those are akin to helper functions that make our code readable.

These comparisons may be somewhat contrived but I felt that looking at maths from the perspective of a programmer makes math less of a pool of symbols and more like recipes and building blocks. 
Solving math problems also became less of a chore, though not any less challenging.

## Computer science and coding

However, there is a distinction between being good at computer science and programming. 
Interestingly and anecdotally, there are also plenty of people who are great at solving computer science and math problems, but are not able to code well.

I guess this is because while computer science concepts are implemented in code, the mental faculties required to solve problems are completely different from those for coding.
A recent [study](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/brain-waves/202003/learning-code-requires-language-skills-not-math) has shown that predisposition to language is actually a better predictor of programming ability than math.

## Levels

With all that said I think that given computer science's roots in mathematics, many believe that math is on a higher plane and proficiency in math equates to proficiency in coding.

While they are very related, I feel that these two subjects are separate and it's perfectly fine to not be good in both :)
